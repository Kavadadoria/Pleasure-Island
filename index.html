Welcome to Pleasure Island, North Carolina!

Pleasure Island is a coastal barrier island off the coast of Southeastern North Carolina,south of Wilmington located in New Hanover County's Federal Point Township.

The island is home to the coastal tourist towns of: Carolina Beach and Kure Beach, as well as the annexed settlements of Wilmington Beach and Han by Beach.

Corncake Inlet (New Inlet) separated the southern end of Pleasure Island from Bald Head Island (Smith Island) until Hurricane Bonnie shoaled and closed the inlet in 1998, making Pleasure Island and Bald Head Island no longer independent islands.

Carolina Beach Inlet is now in the north, the Cape Fear River is in the west, Atlantic Ocean is in the east, and Long Bay (Atlantic Ocean) is in the south. It is 17 miles long, with a width of 2 miles in the far north, 3 miles in the far south, and around 12 miles in the middle. "The point," or headland, known as Cape Fear, is located on the southeastern tip of the merged island. The Frying Pan Shoals, which jut 30 miles into the Atlantic Ocean southeast of the point, are shallow and dangerous. The shoal (or sandbar) is formed by the meeting of Atlantic Ocean Bay and Long Bay longshore currents, as well as the discharge from the Cape Fear River.

Pleasure Island refers to the merged island's northern half, while Bald Head Island refers to the combined island's southern half. Pleasure Island's northern border is formed by Snows Cut. Only at low tide is the rock pile in the foreground visible. Prior to the building of Snows Cut, a man-made canal in 1931, the area that currently makes up Pleasure Island was part of a peninsula known as Federal Point, which spanned from Myrtle Grove to Fort Fisher. At the southern end of the peninsula, on the outskirts of New Inlet, the Federal Point Light previously existed.

North Carolina Beach

The "island" began as the lower tip of the Federal Point Peninsula, which was originally confined in the south by the New Inlet break in the barrier islands. This inlet was "new" since it was formed by a huge storm in September of 1761. New Inlet was really the preferred entrance to the Cape Fear River for ships going for Wilmington for decades afterward, since it provided a deeper, shorter, and less perilous path than the Bald Head/Old Inlet Channel at the Cape Fear's mouth. Blockade runners used the New Inlet route frequently during the Civil War, which led to the construction of Confederate Fort Fisher at this location.
